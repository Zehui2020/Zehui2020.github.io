Common Percussion Instruments

The Drum Kit

The drum kit's origins can be traced back to the late 1800s. During this time, individual percussion instruments like bass drums, snare drums, and cymbals were arranged in marching bands and orchestras, but they were not yet combined into a single instrument setup. As times change and more genres appear, the drum kit we know of today is made!

The Stylophone

The stylophone, a pocket-sized wonder, brings a playful twist to the world of music. Invented in the 1960s, it earned fame as the "pocket electronic organ" due to its unique playability using a stylus. With a simple tap on the metal keys, it unleashes an array of electronic sounds, making it a favorite among musicians and enthusiasts alike. From David Bowie's iconic "Space Oddity" to modern-day melodies, the stylophone's charmingly retro tones continue to inspire creativity and add a touch of nostalgia to the musical landscape.

Less Common Percussion Instruments

Kalimba

The origins of the kalimba can be traced back to ancient African civilizations, dating back thousands of years! The Kalimba is an extremely unique instrument and is criminally underrated. It has a soft and mellow sound to it that is soothing to listen to. The melancholic sound evokes strong emotions during emotional scenes and is one of the most popular instruments used to create game music when depicting a flashback or a sad scene.

Listen

Sleigh Bells

The sleigh bell holds cultural significance in various regions around the world. In some European countries, such as Germany and Austria, the sound of sleigh bells is associated with the arrival of Saint Nicholas or other gift-bringing figures during the holiday season. In North America, the sound of sleigh bells is often associated with sleigh rides and is frequently depicted in Christmas songs and stories.

Listen

Conga

The conga drum's origins can be traced back to Africa, where similar drums were played as part of traditional music and dance rituals. These drums were brought to the Americas through the transatlantic slave trade, with enslaved Africans preserving and adapting their musical traditions in their new cultural contexts.
